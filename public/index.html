What if...

We elected our MPs the same way we elect our MEPs?

This site currently contains 2017 election data.

The way we elect our MPs is a system called First Past The Post. Under First Past The Post, a single winner in each individual constituency gets to represent that constituency in Westminster. When Parliament is formed, this means lots of small victories in constituencies can mean big results for one party, and the overall makeup of parliament does not reflect the way the country as a whole voted. For more information about why this is bad, you can read my Note on Democracy on my blog.

There are, of course, many other ways of running elections and working out who is the winner. The two most common variations are one where you elect more than one person, and the votes are distributed to make the makeup of those elected match the proportions of the vote. The other variation is where you can rank the candidates, so a candidate must receive 50% of the votes to win. If no candidate has received 50% of the vote, then the candidate with the least votes is eliminated, and all the votes that went to that candidate are redistributed to the remaining candidates based on the next order in the rank for each voter. This continues until one candidate receives a majority of the vote.

There is history in the UK for these variations. Until 1950, some constituencies could send more than one MP to parliament, and we have run elections in the past where candidates could be ranked. The three main voting systems to think about are:

  • The Alternative Vote: where you elect a single MP, but rank your choices. We had a referendum on this in 2011, but only 32% of voters backed the change to this system.
  • Single Transferrable Vote: this is a combination of ranking, and electing multiple members. It is one of the most used election systems in the world, and is used in Northern Ireland for EU, local and assembly elections, as well as in Scotland for local elections.
  • D'Hondt method: this is where you can elect multiple members, but you only choose one. This is how England, Wales and Scotland elect MEPs.

This is an experiment where we consider what might happen if we elected our MPs the same way we elected our MEPs. We have the data we need to simulate this, because the method of making a selection is the same, but instead of having 1 MP to represent an area, we can imagine having many. In this experiment, I've combined our existing constituency boundaries into new constituencies, which overall elect 600 MPs to Parliament (the number proposed under the most recent electoral reform), but each constituency elects on average 8 MPs. In theory, this should result in a more representative parliament, but one where coalition governments become common, as it makes it harder for a government to get a majority, unless they also get a majority of the votes, which rarely happens.

There are, of course, caveats. We can assume some people may vote differently if the system was different, there may be less tactical voting, for example. The boundaries I've crudely drawn are simply the same as existing constituencies added together, but in the real world, a lot more care and attention would go into the boundaries. I've also invented some new names, and I didn't put too much thought into it, so please don't complain too much if the name doesn't make sense.
